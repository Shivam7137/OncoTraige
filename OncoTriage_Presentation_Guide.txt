ONCOTRIAGE AI: TECHNICAL ARCHITECTURE & PRESENTATION GUIDE 
================================================================================
Target Audience: Computer Science / Data Science Peers & Judges
Goal: Defend technical choices, explain model architecture, and detail the 
mathematics behind the machine learning pipeline and explainability layers.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY & PROBLEM SPACE
-------------------------------------------------
The challenge in computational pathology isn't just "building an accurate model." 
It's building a model that is:
1. Highly Sensitive (Optimized for Recall over Precision).
2. Mathematically Calibrated (Outputs true probabilities, not just binary classifications).
3. Interpretable (Avoids black-box diagnostics via explainable AI).
4. Self-Auditing (Knows when its own predictions are unreliable).

OncoTriage AI solves this by leveraging the Breast Cancer Wisconsin Diagnostic 
dataset (569 samples, 30 continuous features) to build an advanced Random Forest 
triage engine. It uses Platt Scaling for probability calibration, SHAP (Shapley 
Additive exPlanations) for interpretability, and a custom trinary "Diagnostic 
Reliability Score" to flag out-of-distribution or uncertain cases.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. PIPELINE ARCHITECTURE (PHASE BY PHASE)
-------------------------------------------------

PHASE 1: Exploratory Data Analysis & The Multicollinearity Decision
-------------------------------------------------------------------
We performed standard EDA (KDE plots, correlation heatmaps) and identified severe 
multicollinearity. Many features (like `radius_mean` and `perimeter_mean`) had 
Pearson correlation coefficients |r| > 0.95.

Initial Instinct: Use Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) to drop perfectly collinear 
features, reducing the feature space from 30 to 17. 
Final Decision: WE REJECTED VIF. 
Why? Dropping features is necessary for parametric models (like Logistic 
Regression) because multicollinearity inflates the variance of coefficients, 
ruining interpretability. However, Non-Parametric Ensemble models (like Random 
Forest) are immune to multicollinearity. When a decision tree encounters highly 
correlated features, it simply splits the Gini impurity randomly between them. 
By *keeping* all 30 features, we allow the Random Forest to capture non-linear 
biological nuances (e.g., the differential micro-geometry between convex area 
and jagged perimeter) that VIF would have blindly destroyed.

PHASE 2: Preprocessing (The "Do Nothing" Approach)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
In traditional ML pipelines, it is standard practice to apply StandardScaler (x - μ / σ) 
and Winsorization (clipping outliers at the 99th percentile). 

Final Decision: WE REJECTED SCALING AND OUTLIER CLIPPING.
Why no scaling? Decision Trees partition the feature space using orthogonal 
splits (e.g., x > 5). They do not compute Euclidean distances (like KNN or SVMs) 
or perform gradient descent (like Neural Networks). Therefore, they are entirely 
invariant to monotonic transformations. 
Clinical Benefit: By feeding the model raw data, our SHAP explainer dashboard 
outputs actual clinical units (e.g., Area = 850 mm², Smoothness = 0.11) instead 
of meaningless normalized z-scores (Area = 1.4σ).

Why no clipping? In oncology data, statistical "outliers" are not measurement 
errors; they are literal malignant tumors. A bizarrely large, highly irregular 
cell nucleus *is* the signal. Clipping outliers at the 99th percentile literally 
means capping the severity of the worst cancer cases, artificially blinding the model.

PHASE 3: Model Engineering (Random Forest & Calibration)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Model Selection: We selected a Random Forest Classifier (`n_estimators=500`, 
`max_features='sqrt'`).
Why not Neural Networks? For tabular, structured data with continuous features, 
tree-based ensembles (RF/XGBoost) consistently outperform deep learning 
(Grinsztajn et al., 2022). They also allow for exact tree-based SHAP calculations 
(`TreeExplainer`), which is O(TLD^2) and significantly faster/more accurate than 
the KernelExplainer approximations required for Neural Nets.

The Class Imbalance Problem:
The dataset is imbalanced (63% Benign / 37% Malignant). A naive model optimizing 
for standard Accuracy will naturally bias toward predicting "Benign."
Solution: We injected severe Class Weights (`{0: 1, 1: 4}`). During training, 
the Gini impurity loss function was mathematically altered to penalize a False 
Negative (missing cancer) 400% more than a False Positive (false alarm). 
This shifted the hyperplane to aggressively optimize for Sensitivity (Recall).

Platt Scaling (Calibration):
Random Forests do not output true mathematical probabilities; they output "vote 
fractions" (e.g., 80% of trees voted Malignant). This curve is usually sigmoid-
distorted, meaning 0.8 vote fraction does not equal 80% real-world likelihood.
Solution: We wrapped the Random Forest in `CalibratedClassifierCV` using method='sigmoid' 
(Platt Scaling). This fits a logistic regression model over the Random Forest's 
outputs via Cross-Validation. 
Result: The model's `predict_proba()` now outputs true calibrated probabilities, 
allowing us to safely implement the interactive Decision Threshold Slider in the UI.

PHASE 4: Interpretable AI & Diagnostic Reliability 
-------------------------------------------------------------------
1. SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations):
We use Game Theory physics to tear open the Random Forest black box. SHAP 
computes the exact marginal contribution of each physical feature to the final 
probability. We expose the top 6 "Risk Drivers" (e.g., "Radius Worst +12%") to 
the clinician so they can verify the AI's logic against their own pathology slides.

2. Custom Diagnostic Reliability Score:
Because ML models fail silently on Out-Of-Distribution (OOD) data, we engineered 
a bespoke metric that scores the AI's "Confidence" on a 0-1 scale. It is a 
weighted ensemble of three sub-metrics:
   a) Probability Margin (50% weight): Measures absolute distance from the 
      decision threshold. (Is the prediction safely at 0.05 or 0.95, or 
      dangerously hovering at 0.49?)
   b) SHAP Spread Entropy (30% weight): Calculates the Shannon Entropy of the 
      normalized SHAP values. If entropy is low, it means the model is irrationally 
      obsessing over one single feature. If entropy is high, it means the model 
      made a balanced decision looking at multiple features.
   c) Leaf Consensus (20% weight): We look at the actual predictions of all 500 
      individual Decision Trees. If the trees are split 51% to 49%, the Random Forest 
      is essentially guessing. We measure the variance of the internal estimators.

If the final score drops below a dynamic threshold, the UI throws a "LOW RELIABILITY" 
warning, legally and practically acting as a human-in-the-loop fallback mechanism.

3. Q&A CHEAT SHEET (Technical Defense)
-------------------------------------------------

Q: Your model has False Positives (low Specificity). Why is that acceptable?
A: We are designing a Triage system, not an Oracle. In medical diagnostics, the 
cost matrix is completely asymmetric. A False Positive costs a few hundred dollars 
for an unnecessary secondary biopsy. A False Negative costs a human life. By using 
4:1 class weights and dynamic thresholding, we mathematically accept a drop in 
Specificity to guarantee near-perfect Sensitivity (Recall) — achieving 0.976 to 
1.000 Sensitivity on the test sets.

Q: Why didn't you use XGBoost or LightGBM instead of Random Forest?
A: XGBoost is a boosting algorithm, which iteratively learns from the residuals 
of previous trees. While highly performant, it is significantly more prone to 
overfitting on small datasets (n=569) than Bagging algorithms (Random Forest), 
which train trees independently on bootstrapped samples. Given the extreme 
severity of medical data, the generalization robustness of Random Forest outweighed 
the marginal 0.5% AUC lift XGBoost might have provided.

Q: How do you handle Data Leakage?
A: We handle data leakage at the absolute root. The `p2_` script executes the 
`train_test_split` before *any* other code runs. Scaling, imputation (if we had it), 
Platt Scaling cross-validation, and SHAP TreeExplainer generation are strictly 
bound to the Training Set. The Test Set is isolated and locked until final 
inference, ensuring our Brier Scores and AUC metrics are totally unpolluted.

Q: How is the Streamlit UI so fast if you're executing a 500-tree ensemble?
A: Pre-computation matrices. The interactive threshold slider does not trigger 
model inference. The 500-tree RF and Platt Calibrator are executed exactly once 
on the backend to generate a NumPy contiguous array (`rf_y_prob_test.npy`) containing 
the raw probabilities. When the user slides the threshold from 0.50 to 0.25, the 
frontend is executing a vectorized boolean operation (`y_prob >= threshold`) in 
microseconds, yielding O(1) latency for UI metric recalculations.
